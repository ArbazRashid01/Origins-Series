# Season 08 · Episode 32
# The Boy Who Killed a Giant
### Talut, Jalut (Goliath), and the Rise of Dawud

> *"How many a small company has overcome a large company by the permission of Allah. And Allah is with the patient."*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Al-Baqarah (2:249)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

After Musa, the Children of Israel went through generations of ups and downs. This chapter picks up their story when they demanded a king to lead them in battle against a tyrant. Allah appointed **Talut (Saul)**, tested the army at a river, and through that army a young shepherd named **Dawud (David), peace be upon him**, felled the giant **Jalut (Goliath)** — beginning Dawud's rise to prophethood and kingship. It introduces Season 08's twin prophet-kings: Dawud and his son Sulayman.

> 📖 **Method note:** The story is in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:246-251), with Dawud's qualities across Surah Sad (38), Al-Anbiya (21), and Saba (34). We build from the Qur'an and authentic Sunnah.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

Generations after Musa, the Children of Israel — oppressed and driven from their homes — asked their prophet (traditionally **Samuel**) to **appoint a king** so they could fight in the cause of Allah. Allah chose **Talut (Saul)**, a man of knowledge and physical strength but not of noble wealth — and the people objected, thinking kingship should go to the rich. Their prophet corrected them: Allah chose Talut for his *capability and knowledge*, not his wealth — a sign being the return of the sacred **Ark (Tabut)** carried by angels.

Talut led the army out and **tested them at a river**: whoever drank freely from it was not with him; only those who refrained (or took a single handful) passed. Most failed — only a small band of true believers remained. When they faced **Jalut (Goliath)** and his mighty army, the faithful few prayed for patience and victory. Among Talut's soldiers was the young **Dawud**, who **killed Jalut** — and Allah then "gave Dawud kingship and wisdom (prophethood) and taught him what He willed." Thus a shepherd boy became a prophet-king. The chapter explores the demand for a king, the river-test of sincerity, the triumph of the patient few, and Dawud's God-given gifts: a melodious voice for the **Zabur (Psalms)**, the **softening of iron**, and wise judgment.

## 3. The Story

### 3.1 The Demand for a King and the Choice of Talut

> ✅ **Surah Al-Baqarah (2:246-247):** The chiefs of the Israelites asked their prophet to appoint a king so they could fight for Allah's cause (having been expelled from their homes). When commanded to fight, many then **turned away** — exposing that their resolve was weaker than their request. Allah appointed **Talut**; the people objected: "How can he have kingship over us when we are more worthy of it, and he has not been given abundance of wealth?" The prophet replied: *"Indeed, Allah has chosen him over you and has increased him abundantly in knowledge and stature (*al-'ilm wal-jism*). And Allah gives His sovereignty to whom He wills."*
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** A foundational lesson on **leadership**: the people equated worthiness with **wealth and lineage**; Allah measured it by **knowledge and capability** (*'ilm* and bodily/practical strength). Leadership is a trust given to the *competent and knowledgeable*, not automatically to the rich or well-born. (This echoes "the strong and trustworthy," S7E27.) The sign confirming Talut was the return of the **Ark (Tabut)** — a sacred chest containing relics from the households of Musa and Harun — *carried by the angels* (2:248).

### 3.2 The River-Test of Sincerity

> ✅ **Surah Al-Baqarah (2:249)**
>
> **Arabic:** فَلَمَّا فَصَلَ طَالُوتُ بِالْجُنُودِ قَالَ إِنَّ اللَّهَ مُبْتَلِيكُم بِنَهَرٍ فَمَن شَرِبَ مِنْهُ فَلَيْسَ مِنِّي وَمَن لَّمْ يَطْعَمْهُ فَإِنَّهُ مِنِّي إِلَّا مَنِ اغْتَرَفَ غُرْفَةً بِيَدِهِ
>
> **Translation:** "And when Talut went forth with the soldiers, he said, 'Indeed, Allah will test you with a river. Whoever drinks from it is not of me, and whoever does not taste it is of me, except one who takes a sip with his hand.' But they drank from it, except a few of them..."
>
> **Tafsir:** Talut tested his army's discipline and trust with a simple command: don't drink freely from the river (a single handful was permitted). **Most failed** — overwhelmed by thirst and desire, they drank deeply, disqualifying themselves. Only **a small, disciplined, faithful band** obeyed. The lesson: **quantity is not strength; sincerity and discipline are.** An army of the half-hearted is worse than a small band of the committed. This screening separated the truly faithful from the merely willing.

### 3.3 "How Many a Small Company..." — The Faith of the Few

> ✅ **Surah Al-Baqarah (2:249-250)**
>
> **Translation:** "...When he had crossed it with those who believed, they said, 'There is no power for us today against Jalut and his soldiers.' But those who were certain they would meet Allah said, **'How many a small company has overcome a large company by the permission of Allah. And Allah is with the patient.'** And when they went forth to [face] Jalut and his soldiers, they said, 'Our Lord, pour upon us patience and plant firmly our feet and give us victory over the disbelieving people.'"
>
> **Tafsir:** Even among the faithful few, some wavered at the sight of Jalut's vast army. But the truly certain (*alladhina yazunnuna annahum mulaqu-llah* — "those certain they would meet Allah") spoke the timeless truth: **victory is by Allah's permission, not by numbers** — "how many a small company has overcome a large one." Their du'a (2:250) — "pour upon us patience, plant our feet firm, grant us victory" — became a treasured supplication for facing overwhelming odds (recited by the Prophet ﷺ and the believers at Badr, Season 13).

### 3.4 Dawud Kills the Giant

> ✅ **Surah Al-Baqarah (2:251)**
>
> **Translation:** "So they defeated them by the permission of Allah, and **Dawud killed Jalut**, and Allah gave him the kingship and wisdom (prophethood) and taught him from that which He willed. And if it were not for Allah checking [some] people by means of others, the earth would have been corrupted; but Allah is full of bounty to the worlds."
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir):** The young **Dawud** — a shepherd in Talut's army, not a famous warrior — **slew the giant Jalut** (by tradition, with a stone from his sling, though the Qur'an gives only the fact). This single act launched Dawud's destiny: Allah granted him **kingship** (he later succeeded Talut) **and prophethood and knowledge.** The verse closes with a profound principle of divine wisdom: Allah "checks people by means of others" (*daf' Allah an-nas ba'dahum bi-ba'd*) — He uses the righteous to restrain corruption and tyranny; otherwise the earth would be ruined. The defeat of a tyrant by a faithful few is part of how Allah maintains balance in the world.

### 3.5 Dawud's God-Given Gifts

Dawud became one of the great prophet-kings, granted unique favours:

> ✅ **Surah Sad (38:18-20):** The **mountains and birds** would glorify Allah *along with* Dawud — his voice for praising Allah was so beautiful that creation joined him. Allah "strengthened his kingdom and gave him wisdom and decisive speech."
> ✅ **Surah Saba (34:10-11):** "We gave Dawud bounty from Us... and We made iron pliable for him" — Allah **softened iron** in his hands so he could fashion coats of armour. (He worked with his own hands — see §5.)
> ✅ **The Zabur (Psalms):** Allah gave Dawud the **Zabur** (Psalms) — *"and to Dawud We gave the Zabur"* (4:163; 17:55) — a revealed scripture of praise and devotion.

## 5. The Themes

1. **Leadership belongs to the capable, not the wealthy** (2:247). Allah chose Talut for knowledge and strength over riches and lineage — a rebuke to every society that equates worthiness with wealth.
2. **Sincerity is screened by tests.** The river separated the committed few from the half-hearted many (2:249). Allah filters His servants through trials; only the disciplined and trusting pass.
3. **Victory is by Allah's permission, not numbers** (2:249). "How many a small company has overcome a large one" — the believer never despairs at being outnumbered, for "Allah is with the patient."
4. **Allah restrains corruption through the righteous** (2:251). The struggle of the faithful against tyranny is how Allah preserves the earth from ruin.
5. **Even a prophet-king works with his hands** (Bukhari 2072) — the dignity of honest labour and humble living.
6. **Worship in moderation, sustainably** (Bukhari 3417) — Dawud's fasting (alternate days) is "the most beloved" because it is balanced and lasting, not extreme.

## 6. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"Kingship should go to the wealthy or noble-born."**
> ✅ Allah explicitly chose Talut for **knowledge and capability**, not wealth (2:247), correcting the people's assumption. Competence and integrity, not riches, qualify a leader.

> ❌ **"A big army guarantees victory."**
> ✅ "How many a small company has overcome a large one by Allah's permission" (2:249). Victory is from Allah; numbers are secondary to faith, discipline, and patience.

> ❌ **"David (Dawud) in Islam is the same flawed figure as in some other traditions (e.g., the Bathsheba narrative)."**
> ✅ In Islam, Dawud is a **noble prophet** — Islam rejects the sinful, scandalous portrayals found in some Israelite narratives (see §7). He was a devout, just, hard-working prophet-king.

> ❌ **"A prophet-king living off his own labour is beneath his station."**
> ✅ The opposite — the Prophet ﷺ **praised** Dawud for eating from his own hands' work (Bukhari 2072). Honest labour and humility honour any station.

## 7. Weak / Isra'iliyyat Narrations (Labelled)

---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 1 — The "Dawud and Bathsheba / Uriah" story**

- **Narration:** That Dawud schemed to take the wife of one of his soldiers (the Biblical Bathsheba/Uriah narrative), connected by some to the "two disputants" in Surah Sad (38:21-25).
- **Source:** **Isra'iliyyat** imported into some tafsir; the Qur'an gives **no such story.**
- **Why rejected:** It contradicts the *'ismah* (protection from major sin) of prophets and slanders a noble prophet. The Qur'an's "two disputants" episode (38:21-25) is about a *test* and Dawud's swift repentance over a matter of judgment — **not** adultery or murder. Ibn Kathir and others firmly reject the Bathsheba slander.
- **Rely on it?** ❌ **NO.** Reject this entirely. Dawud was a righteous prophet.
---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 2 — The sling-stone details, Jalut's exact size, the number of Talut's army**

- **Narration:** That Dawud used five stones, the giant's precise height (cubits), the exact count of those who passed the river (often "313," matching Badr).
- **Source:** Tradition; some details (like 313) come from athar.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ The Qur'an gives the *fact* that Dawud killed Jalut and that "a few" passed the river; specific numbers/details are traditional. (The "313" parallel to Badr is noted in some reports but treat as supplementary.)

## 8. Important People

**Dawud (David), peace be upon him:** The shepherd boy who killed Jalut and became a **prophet-king**; given the Zabur, a beautiful voice, softened iron, wise judgment, and decisive speech. Model of devout, balanced worship and honest labour.

**Talut (Saul):** The king Allah chose for knowledge and capability over wealth; led the river-test and the army against Jalut.

**Jalut (Goliath):** The tyrant-giant defeated by young Dawud — symbol of overwhelming worldly power overcome by faith.

**The prophet (traditionally Samuel):** Who conveyed Allah's choice of Talut and the sign of the Ark.

## 11. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Who chose Talut as king, and why did people object?**
A: Allah chose him; the people objected because he wasn't wealthy. Their prophet explained Allah chose him for **knowledge and capability** (2:247).

**Q: What was the river-test?**
A: Talut told the army not to drink freely from a river (a handful was allowed). Most drank and failed; only a disciplined few passed — separating the sincere (2:249).

**Q: Did Dawud really kill Goliath with a sling?**
A: The Qur'an states Dawud killed Jalut (2:251); the sling-stone detail is from tradition, plausible but not in the Qur'anic text.

**Q: Is the "David and Bathsheba" story true in Islam?**
A: **No** — it's an Isra'iliyyat slander rejected by scholars. Dawud was a noble, sinless-of-major-sin prophet (§7).

**Q: What were Dawud's special gifts?**
A: Prophethood and kingship, the **Zabur (Psalms)**, a beautiful voice (mountains and birds glorified Allah with him), **softened iron** for armour-making, and wise, decisive judgment.
