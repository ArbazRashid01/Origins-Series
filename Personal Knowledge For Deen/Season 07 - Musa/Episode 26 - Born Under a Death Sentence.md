# Season 07 · Episode 26
# Born Under a Death Sentence
### The Baby in the River, and the Tyrant Who Feared a Prophecy

> *"And We inspired the mother of Musa, 'Suckle him; but when you fear for him, cast him into the river and do not fear and do not grieve. Indeed, We will return him to you and make him one of the messengers.'"*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Al-Qasas (28:7)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

We now arrive at the most-mentioned prophet in the Qur'an: **Musa (Moses), peace be upon him** — referenced in over 70 places, his story the most detailed of all. Generations after Yusuf brought the Children of Israel into Egypt with honour, a tyrant — **Fir'awn (Pharaoh)** — enslaved them and, fearing a prophecy, ordered the killing of their infant boys. This chapter covers that oppression, the miraculous saving of baby Musa in a basket on the Nile, and Allah's astonishing plan to raise the future liberator *inside Pharaoh's own palace.*

> 📖 **Method note:** Musa's story spans many surahs (Al-Baqarah, Al-A'raf, Ta-Ha, Ash-Shu'ara, Al-Qasas, etc.). Surah Al-Qasas (28) gives the fullest birth-narrative. We build from the Qur'an and authentic Sunnah.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

By the time of Musa, the **Children of Israel** (descendants of Ya'qub/Yusuf) had become a large, oppressed underclass in Egypt. **Pharaoh** ruled as a tyrant who "exalted himself in the land and made its people into factions, oppressing one group" (28:4). Warned (by a dream or soothsayers, per tradition) that a boy from the Israelites would end his rule, he instituted a horrific policy: **slaughtering the newborn sons** of the Israelites while sparing the girls — genocide by population control.

Into this terror, Musa was born. Allah **inspired his mother** with an extraordinary command: nurse him, and when you fear discovery, **place him in the river** — with the promise, "do not fear, do not grieve; We will return him to you and make him a messenger." With breaking heart and total trust, she set her baby afloat on the Nile in a basket. By Allah's design, the basket drifted to **Pharaoh's own palace**, where it was found by **Pharaoh's wife (Asiyah)**, a righteous woman, who loved the child and persuaded Pharaoh to keep him. Then — the perfect twist of divine planning — the baby refused all wet-nurses until his **own mother** was brought (via his watching sister) to nurse him. Thus Allah **returned Musa to his mother**, paid, safe, and raised the future destroyer of Pharaoh *in Pharaoh's own house, on Pharaoh's own wealth.*

This chapter explores the oppression, the mother's faith, the river, and the breathtaking irony of Allah's plan — a profound lesson that no tyrant's scheme can overturn the decree of Allah.

## 3. Background: Egypt, the Israelites, and Pharaoh

Centuries after Yusuf, the Israelites in Egypt had multiplied but lost their status, becoming an enslaved, oppressed people. The ruler — **Fir'awn (Pharaoh)** is a *title* for the kings of Egypt, not a personal name — embodied tyranny at its most extreme:

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:4):** "Indeed, Pharaoh exalted himself in the land and made its people into factions, oppressing a sector among them, slaughtering their [newborn] sons and keeping their females alive. Indeed, he was of the corrupters."
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir):** Pharaoh's regime combined **class oppression** ("made its people into factions"), **slavery**, and **genocide** (killing male infants). Tradition holds he acted on a warning — a dream or a soothsayer's prophecy — that a male child of the Israelites would bring down his kingdom. So he tried to **kill the threat before it was born** — the futile arrogance of a tyrant who imagines he can outmaneuver destiny. (Some reports say he alternated the killing year by year, which is how Harun, Musa's older brother, survived.)

## 4. The Story

### 4.1 The Mother's Inspiration and the River

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:7)**
>
> **Arabic:** وَأَوْحَيْنَا إِلَىٰ أُمِّ مُوسَىٰ أَنْ أَرْضِعِيهِ ۖ فَإِذَا خِفْتِ عَلَيْهِ فَأَلْقِيهِ فِي الْيَمِّ وَلَا تَخَافِي وَلَا تَحْزَنِي ۖ إِنَّا رَادُّوهُ إِلَيْكِ وَجَاعِلُوهُ مِنَ الْمُرْسَلِينَ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wa awhayna ila ummi Musa an ardi'ih, fa-idha khifti 'alayhi fa-alqihi fi al-yammi wa la takhafi wa la tahzani, inna radduhu ilayki wa ja'iluhu mina al-mursalin.*
>
> **Translation:** "And We inspired the mother of Musa, 'Suckle him; but when you fear for him, cast him into the river and do not fear and do not grieve. Indeed, We will return him to you and make him one of the messengers.'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** The "inspiration" (*wahy*) here is not prophethood but a divinely-placed certainty/instruction in her heart. Consider the **magnitude of her faith**: she was commanded to do the thing that *appears* most dangerous — placing her infant in a *river* — to *save* him from the danger on land. It defies all human logic, yet she obeyed, trusting Allah's promise. This is the supreme lesson: **sometimes Allah's command leads through what looks like risk, but obedience is the true safety.** Allah gave her two commands ("suckle," "cast him") and two prohibitions ("do not fear, do not grieve") and two glad tidings ("We will return him," "make him a messenger") — a complete container of reassurance.

### 4.2 Found by the Enemy — and Loved

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:8-9)**
>
> **Translation:** "And the family of Pharaoh picked him up [out of the river] so that he would become to them an enemy and a [cause of] grief. Indeed, Pharaoh and Haman and their soldiers were deliberate sinners. And the wife of Pharaoh said, 'A comfort of the eye for me and for you. Do not kill him; perhaps he may benefit us, or we may adopt him as a son.' And they perceived not [what was to come]."
>
> **Tafsir:** The supreme irony of divine planning: the basket floated **straight to the palace of the very tyrant** hunting Israelite babies. Allah states the purpose with devastating understatement — they took him in "*so that he would become to them an enemy and grief*" — the man who would dismantle Pharaoh's kingdom was lovingly carried *into* that kingdom's heart by Pharaoh's own household. **Asiyah, Pharaoh's righteous wife**, fell in love with the baby and shielded him, persuading Pharaoh to keep him. *"And they perceived not"* — the tyrant, for all his power and spies, had no idea he was raising his own undoing.

### 4.3 Returned to His Mother

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:10-13):** Musa's mother's heart "became empty" with grief; she nearly disclosed the secret but Allah strengthened her heart. She sent **his sister** to follow the basket from afar. Musa **refused to nurse from any wet-nurse** (by Allah's design). His watching sister then offered: "Shall I direct you to a household that will care for him?" — and brought **his own mother.** So Allah **returned Musa to his mother** to nurse and raise — *paid by the palace* — "so that she might be comforted and not grieve, and that she would know the promise of Allah is true" (28:13).
>
> **Tafsir:** The plan is perfect: the mother who cast her baby into the river out of obedience now nurses him **in safety, with a salary from Pharaoh's treasury**, her son protected by the palace that should have killed him. Allah's promise — "We will return him to you" — was fulfilled exactly. *No scheme of the tyrant could prevent it; in fact, the tyrant funded it.*

Living in the household of history's archetypal tyrant, she kept pure faith — and Allah praises her in the Qur'an (66:11) as an example for *all believers*: "My Lord, build for me a house near You in Paradise, and save me from Pharaoh and his deeds." Her finding and protecting baby Musa was the first thread of her noble story.

## 6. The Theme: No Scheme Outmaneuvers Allah's Decree

This chapter is the Qur'an's masterclass on the **futility of fighting destiny**:

- Pharaoh killed thousands of babies to stop *one* prophesied boy — and that boy was raised **in his own palace, by his own wealth, protected by his own wife.**
- Every precaution the tyrant took became the **vehicle** of the outcome he feared.
- The mother's act of apparent abandonment (the river) was the act of **salvation**.

> 💡 The Qur'an's point: *"They plan, and Allah plans, and Allah is the best of planners"* (cf. 8:30). Human power, however vast, cannot overturn what Allah decrees. The mightiest tyrant in the story is, from the first page, already defeated — he just doesn't know it.

---

## 7. Lessons

1. **Obey Allah even when His command seems to lead through danger.** Musa's mother put her baby in a river on Allah's word — and it was the only thing that saved him. True safety is in obedience, not in what *looks* safe.
2. **"Do not fear and do not grieve."** Allah's reassurance to a terrified mother is His reassurance to every believer facing the impossible. Trust His promise.
3. **No tyrant can defeat Allah's plan.** Pharaoh's genocide, spies, and power could not stop one baby Allah willed to raise. Oppressors are never as in control as they appear.
4. **Allah provides from where you least expect.** Musa was protected and funded by the very enemy hunting him. Allah's provision (*rizq*) reaches us through unimaginable channels.
5. **Faith can flourish even in a tyrant's house.** Asiyah, surrounded by Pharaoh's evil, became one of the greatest women in history. Your environment does not determine your faith; your choices do.

## 8. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"'Pharaoh' was the man's name."**
> ✅ *Fir'awn* (Pharaoh) is a **title** for Egypt's kings (like "Caesar" or "King"). The Qur'an uses the title; it does not give his personal name.

> ❌ **"Musa's mother abandoned him recklessly."**
> ✅ She acted on **divine inspiration** with total trust (28:7), and it was the means of his salvation and her reunion with him.

> ❌ **"Asiyah was complicit in Pharaoh's evil because she was his wife."**
> ✅ She is praised by Allah and the Prophet ﷺ as among the **greatest women ever** (66:11; Bukhari 3411); she **rejected** Pharaoh's evil and prayed for deliverance from it. Faith over family/household — again.

## 9. Weak / Isra'iliyyat Narrations (Labelled)

---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 1 — The dream/soothsayer details, the alternating killing years, the names and numbers**

- **Narration:** Detailed accounts of Pharaoh's dream (a fire from Jerusalem consuming Egypt), his astrologers' advice, the exact policy of killing boys in alternate years, the number of babies killed, the names of the midwives.
- **Source:** Isra'iliyyat and tradition; the Qur'an gives the *fact* of the killing and the oppression without these specifics.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ The Qur'anic essentials are certain; the elaborate backstory is unverified tradition.
---
**📖 Note — Which Pharaoh? (academic discussion):** Scholars and historians debate which historical Egyptian king was "the Pharaoh of Musa" (commonly proposed: Ramesses II or his successor Merneptah). The Qur'an does not name him, so **no specific identification is part of Islamic belief.** (Notably, the Qur'an says Pharaoh's *body* would be preserved as a sign — 10:92 — discussed in Episode 31.) Treat historical identifications as scholarly hypotheses, not doctrine.

## 10. Important People

**Musa (Moses), peace be upon him:** The future prophet and liberator; one of the five *Ulu al-'Azm*; the most-mentioned prophet in the Qur'an; *Kalim-Allah* ("the one Allah spoke to directly"). Here, a vulnerable newborn under Allah's protection.

**Musa's mother:** A woman of extraordinary faith, who obeyed the hardest command and was rewarded with her son's safe return.

**Musa's sister:** Who watched over the basket and cleverly reunited Musa with his mother. (Tradition names her Maryam/Miriam — not in the Qur'an by name here.)

**Asiyah (Pharaoh's wife):** One of the greatest women in history; she found, loved, and protected baby Musa, and kept pure faith amid tyranny (66:11; Bukhari 3411).

**Pharaoh (Fir'awn):** The tyrant-king whose genocidal scheme to stop a prophecy ironically delivered the prophesied child into his own home.

**Haman:** Pharaoh's chief minister/aide, named in the Qur'an as a partner in his tyranny (28:6, 28:8).

## 13. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Why did Pharaoh kill the baby boys?**
A: He feared a prophecy that an Israelite boy would end his rule, so he tried to eliminate the threat by genocide (28:4) — a futile attempt to outmaneuver Allah's decree.

**Q: Was it safe to put a baby in the Nile?**
A: By human logic, no — but it was Allah's command, and therefore the *only* safe path. Allah carried the basket to the palace and back to the mother (28:7-13).

**Q: Who found Musa?**
A: Pharaoh's household; specifically **Asiyah, Pharaoh's righteous wife**, who loved and protected him (28:9).

**Q: How was Musa reunited with his mother?**
A: He refused all wet-nurses; his sister suggested "a family" who could nurse him — and brought his own mother, who then raised him, paid by the palace (28:12-13).

**Q: Who was the Pharaoh of Musa historically?**
A: Unknown for certain; commonly proposed as Ramesses II or Merneptah, but the Qur'an doesn't name him, so it's not a matter of belief (§9).
