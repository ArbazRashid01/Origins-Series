# Season 07 · Episode 27
# The Prince Who Ran
### The Killing, the Flight to Madyan, and the Years of Waiting

> *"My Lord, indeed I am, for whatever good You would send down to me, in need."*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Al-Qasas (28:24)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

Musa grew up a prince in Pharaoh's palace — but his heart belonged to his oppressed people. This chapter covers the pivotal incident that changed his life: **accidentally killing an Egyptian** while defending an Israelite, his flight from Egypt as a wanted man, his arrival in **Madyan**, the kindness he showed two women at a well, his marriage, and the **ten years** he spent as a humble shepherd — the hidden training that prepared a prince to become a prophet. It bridges his royal upbringing (Episode 26) and his prophetic calling at the burning bush (Episode 28).

---

## 1. Episode Summary

Raised in the palace, Musa grew into a strong young man granted "judgment and knowledge" by Allah (28:14). One day he entered the city and found **two men fighting** — one an Israelite (his people), one an Egyptian (the oppressors). The Israelite cried for help; Musa struck the Egyptian with his fist — and the man **died.** Musa had not intended to kill him; horrified, he recognized it as a grave mistake and immediately **repented**: *"This is from the work of Shaytan... My Lord, I have wronged myself, so forgive me."* Allah forgave him.

The next day, the same Israelite was again in a fight and called for help; when Musa moved to intervene, the man (thinking Musa would strike him) blurted out that Musa was the one who had killed yesterday — exposing the secret. Word reached Pharaoh's court, and a sympathizer **raced to warn Musa** that the chiefs were plotting to kill him. Musa **fled Egypt**, alone, afraid, and praying: *"My Lord, save me from the wrongdoing people."*

He journeyed to **Madyan** (north-west Arabia). Exhausted at a well, he saw two women holding their flocks back while shepherds watered theirs. Out of pure chivalry and compassion, **Musa watered their flock for them**, asking nothing — then withdrew to the shade and made one of the most beautiful, humble du'as in the Qur'an: *"My Lord, I am in need of whatever good You send me."* One of the women returned, "walking with modesty," to invite him: her father wished to reward him. That father (a righteous elder, traditionally identified with the prophet **Shu'ayb** or a man of his people) offered Musa **marriage to one of his daughters** in exchange for **eight (or ten) years of shepherding.** Musa accepted, married, and spent about **ten years** in Madyan — a prince humbled into a shepherd, his character forged in patience, labour, and obscurity, until the day Allah would call him.

## 3. The Story

### 3.1 The Accidental Killing and Immediate Repentance

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:15-16)**
>
> **Translation:** "And he entered the city at a time of inattention of its people and found therein two men fighting: one from his faction (the Israelites) and one from his enemy (the Egyptians). The one from his faction called for help against the one from his enemy, so Musa struck him and [unintentionally] killed him. He said, 'This is from the work of Shaytan. Indeed, he is a manifest, misleading enemy.' He said, 'My Lord, indeed I have wronged myself, so forgive me,' and He forgave him. Indeed, He is the Forgiving, the Merciful."
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** Musa did not *intend* to kill — a single blow from a powerful man proved fatal. Yet note his response, the mark of a righteous soul: he did not justify it ("he deserved it," "he was an oppressor") but immediately recognized the gravity, attributed the *anger* that led to it to Shaytan's influence, **owned his fault** ("I have wronged myself"), and **begged forgiveness** — and Allah forgave him at once. This mirrors Adam (S2E8), not Iblis: ownership and repentance, not arrogance and excuse. (It was, in the categories of later law, closer to an unintended killing in a moment of rage defending the oppressed — a serious matter Musa rightly repented for.)

### 3.2 Exposure and Flight

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:18-21):** The next morning, the same Israelite was again fighting and called Musa for help; as Musa approached angrily, the man — fearing Musa would hit *him* — exposed the secret. The killing became known. A man came **from the far end of the city, running**, warning: "O Musa, the chiefs are conferring to kill you, so leave; I am to you a sincere advisor" (28:20). Musa fled, "fearful and anticipating," praying: *"My Lord, save me from the wrongdoing people"* (28:21).
>
> **Tafsir:** Overnight, Musa went from palace prince to **hunted fugitive.** His flight, alone and afraid into the desert, was the beginning of his stripping-away of privilege — the necessary humbling before prophethood. The "man who ran to warn him" shows that even in a tyrant's city, Allah places hidden allies for the righteous.

### 3.3 Madyan, the Well, and Chivalry

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:22-24)**
>
> **Translation:** "And when he directed himself toward Madyan, he said, 'Perhaps my Lord will guide me to the right way.' And when he came to the well of Madyan, he found there a crowd of people watering [their flocks], and he found aside from them two women holding back [their flock]. He said, 'What is your circumstance?' They said, 'We do not water until the shepherds disperse, and our father is an old man.' So he watered [their flock] for them; then he turned back to the shade and said, **'My Lord, indeed I am, for whatever good You would send down to me, in need.'**"
>
> **Tafsir (as-Sa'di):** Exhausted, hungry, alone, and a fugitive — yet the first thing Musa did was **help two strangers** out of pure decency, asking nothing in return. His character shone in adversity. Then his du'a (28:24) is a model of beautiful humility: he did not *demand* of Allah; he simply confessed his **need** and his openness to "whatever good" Allah would send — a prayer of total dependence and contentment. It was answered almost immediately, in abundance.

### 3.4 Marriage and the Decade of Shepherding

> ✅ **Surah Al-Qasas (28:25-28):** One of the two women returned, "walking with shyness/modesty (*'ala istihya'*)," and said her father invited him to reward him for watering their flock. Musa told the old man his story. One of the daughters suggested hiring Musa, saying: *"O my father, hire him; indeed, the best one to hire is the strong and the trustworthy (*al-qawiyy al-amin*)"* (28:26). The father offered Musa marriage to one of his daughters in return for serving **eight years, or ten if he chose** (28:27). Musa agreed.
>
> **Tafsir:** Two famous lessons emerge:
> - **"The best to hire is the strong and trustworthy" (28:26):** This phrase became a foundational principle in Islam for **selecting people for responsibility** — competence (*quwwah*, strength/ability) **and** integrity (*amanah*, trustworthiness) together. The woman noticed both in Musa (his strength at the well, his trustworthy lowering of his gaze and conduct).
> - **The daughter's modesty (*istihya'*):** Her shy, dignified manner is praised in the Qur'an — a model of decorum.
> Musa then spent about **ten years** as a shepherd in Madyan. This obscure decade — patient labour, simplicity, far from palace luxury — was Allah's *training* of the future prophet. (Tending sheep is noted as a common preparation of prophets — see §5.) The prince was being remade into a shepherd of men.

## 4. Who Was the Old Man of Madyan?

> 📖 **Scholarly discussion:** The Qur'an does not name Musa's father-in-law. Two main views:
> - He was the **Prophet Shu'ayb** (peace be upon him), the prophet sent to Madyan (whose own story is in Surah Al-A'raf, Hud, Ash-Shu'ara). *Held by many,* on the assumption that the righteous elder of Madyan was its prophet.
> - He was **a righteous man of Shu'ayb's people/relatives** (not Shu'ayb himself), since some place Shu'ayb's lifetime earlier.
> **Conclusion:** The Qur'an leaves him unnamed; the "Shu'ayb" identification is traditional and *possible* but not certain. We note both views. *(Madyan itself is the same nation Shu'ayb was sent to — see the cross-reference in §7.)*

> ✅ **On prophets and shepherding (Bukhari 3406):** The Prophet ﷺ said, **"Allah sent no prophet except that he shepherded sheep."** His Companions asked, "Even you?" He said, "Yes, I used to shepherd the sheep of the people of Makkah for a few carats." **Lesson:** Shepherding teaches patience, gentleness, vigilance, and care for the weak and scattered — exactly the qualities of leading people. Musa's decade as a shepherd in Madyan was divine preparation for shepherding a nation out of slavery.

## 6. Lessons

1. **The righteous own their mistakes and repent at once.** Musa's accidental killing was met not with excuses but with "I have wronged myself, forgive me" (28:16) — and Allah forgave. Repentance, not justification, is the believer's reflex.
2. **Adversity reveals (and builds) character.** A hunted, exhausted fugitive still stopped to help two strangers (28:24). Who you are under pressure is who you really are.
3. **"I am in need of whatever good You send."** The model du'a of humility — not demanding specifics, but trusting Allah's choice of what is good. It was answered with shelter, family, and livelihood.
4. **Choose people by strength *and* trustworthiness** (28:26). The Qur'anic standard for any role of responsibility: capability + integrity. Neither alone is enough.
5. **Allah prepares His servants in obscurity.** Ten years of humble shepherding forged the prophet. Your "wilderness years" of unseen struggle may be exactly the training for your purpose.
6. **Modesty and dignity are praised** (28:25) — the woman who approached "with shyness" is honoured in the Qur'an.

## 7. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"Musa was a murderer."**
> ✅ The killing was **unintentional** (a single blow in defense of the oppressed), and Musa **repented immediately** and was **forgiven by Allah** (28:16). He was not a murderer in intent or in Allah's judgment.

> ❌ **"Musa's father-in-law was definitely the Prophet Shu'ayb."**
> ✅ The Qur'an doesn't name him. "Shu'ayb" is a traditional, *possible* identification, not a certainty (§4).

> ❌ **"The years in Madyan were wasted/insignificant."**
> ✅ They were **divine preparation** — the shepherding and simplicity that forged a prince into a prophet (Bukhari 3406).

> ❌ **"It's shameful that a prophet worked as a hired shepherd."**
> ✅ The opposite — **all** prophets shepherded (Bukhari 3406); honest labour is honourable, and humility is a prophetic trait.

## 12. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Did Musa commit murder?**
A: No — it was an **unintentional** killing while defending an oppressed man; Musa repented immediately and Allah forgave him (28:16).

**Q: Where is Madyan?**
A: In north-west Arabia (the region near the Gulf of Aqaba), the homeland of the people of the Prophet Shu'ayb.

**Q: Was his father-in-law the Prophet Shu'ayb?**
A: Possibly — it's a traditional identification, but the Qur'an doesn't name him (§4).

**Q: How long did Musa stay in Madyan?**
A: He agreed to serve eight years, with ten as the more virtuous option; he completed "the more complete of the two terms" (per a hadith) — about ten years.

**Q: Why did Allah make a future prophet work as a shepherd for a decade?**
A: As preparation — shepherding builds patience, gentleness, and care for the vulnerable, the qualities needed to lead a nation (Bukhari 3406).
