# Season 09 · Episode 35
# The Man Who Lost Everything
### Ayyub (Job) and the Patience That Became Legendary

> *"Indeed, We found him patient. What an excellent servant! Indeed, he was one repeatedly turning back [to Allah]."*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Sad (38:44)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

From prophet-kings of power (Season 08), the story turns inward — to a prophet tested not by tyrants but by **catastrophic loss**: **Ayyub (Job), peace be upon him**. He lost his wealth, his children, and his health, suffering for years — yet never complained against Allah, never lost his faith, and became the Qur'an's eternal symbol of *sabr* (patience). This chapter explores his trial, his beautiful, dignified du'a, his restoration, and the deep lessons on patience, gratitude, and the proper way to call upon Allah in suffering.

> 📖 **Method note:** Ayyub's story is brief but profound in the Qur'an (Sad 38:41-44; Al-Anbiya 21:83-84). The Qur'an deliberately omits the gory details that fill Isra'iliyyat — focusing on his *patience* and *restoration*. We follow that focus and flag the embellishments.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

**Ayyub** was a prophet blessed with abundant wealth, a large family, and excellent health — and he was deeply grateful and devout. Then Allah tested him by **stripping it all away**: his wealth vanished, his children died, and his body was struck with a severe, prolonged illness that left him weak and isolated, with — by tradition — only his faithful wife remaining to care for him. The trial lasted **many years**.

Through it all, Ayyub remained **patient and grateful**, never blaming or despairing of Allah. The Qur'an immortalizes his restraint: even in his suffering, his du'a was a model of *adab* (manners) with Allah — he did not demand or complain, but gently said: *"Indeed, adversity has touched me, and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful"* (21:83) — stating his condition and Allah's mercy, without a hint of objection. To Shaytan's whispers (who tried to use the long affliction to break his faith), Ayyub held firm.

Finally, Allah commanded him: *"Strike [the ground] with your foot; this is a [spring] for washing, cool and a drink"* (38:42) — and a healing spring gushed forth that restored his health. Allah **gave him back his family and doubled it**, and made him "a reminder for the worshippers" and an example of *sabr* for all time. The Prophet ﷺ would later cite Ayyub as the standard of patience. This chapter explores the loss, the patience, the perfect du'a, the healing, and the restoration — a masterclass on enduring suffering with faith intact.

## 3. The Story

### 3.1 The Test of Total Loss

> ✅ **Surah Sad (38:41)**
>
> **Translation:** "And remember Our servant Ayyub, when he called to his Lord, 'Indeed, Shaytan has touched me with hardship and torment.'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** Ayyub was a prophet of great blessings — and Allah tested him by removing them all: **wealth, children, and health.** This is one of the profound truths of faith: *trials are not a sign of Allah's anger, but often of His love and His testing of the righteous.* The Prophet ﷺ taught: "The people most severely tested are the prophets, then the next best, then the next... a person is tested according to his faith" (**Jami' at-Tirmidhi 2398**, *hasan/sahih*). Ayyub's catastrophic losses were not punishment but the **trial of a beloved servant**, raising his rank. Note Ayyub attributed the *hardship* to Shaytan's "touch" (the whispers and distress) out of *adab* — never attributing evil directly in a complaining way to Allah, though all is by Allah's decree.

### 3.2 The Patience That Became Proverbial

The defining feature of Ayyub is his **sabr** — patience without complaint:

> ✅ **Surah Sad (38:44):** "...Indeed, We found him patient. What an excellent servant! Indeed, he was one repeatedly turning back [to Allah] (*awwab*)."
> ✅ **Surah Al-Anbiya (21:83):** His du'a in the depth of suffering.
>
> **Tafsir:** Ayyub's patience was so complete that "the patience of Ayyub" became a proverb across civilizations. He did not curse his fate, abandon his worship, or despair of Allah's mercy through *years* of agony. Crucially, his patience was not silent misery but **active turning to Allah** (*awwab* — "ever-returning"). Patience in Islam is not passive resignation; it is **steadfast faith + continued worship + hope in Allah** through the trial.

### 3.3 The Perfect Du'a — A Lesson in Manners With Allah

> ✅ **Surah Al-Anbiya (21:83-84)**
>
> **Arabic:** وَأَيُّوبَ إِذْ نَادَىٰ رَبَّهُ أَنِّي مَسَّنِيَ الضُّرُّ وَأَنتَ أَرْحَمُ الرَّاحِمِينَ ۝ فَاسْتَجَبْنَا لَهُ فَكَشَفْنَا مَا بِهِ مِن ضُرٍّ...
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wa Ayyuba idh nada Rabbahu anni massaniya ad-durru wa anta arhamu ar-rahimin. Fastajabna lahu fakashafna ma bihi min durr...*
>
> **Translation:** "And [mention] Ayyub, when he called to his Lord, 'Indeed, adversity has touched me, and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful.' So We responded to him and removed what afflicted him of adversity..."
>
> **Tafsir (as-Sa'di):** This du'a is a **masterpiece of *adab* (etiquette) with Allah.** Notice what Ayyub did *not* do: he did not say "cure me!" as a demand, did not complain "why me?", did not list his sufferings to provoke pity. He simply **stated his reality gently** ("adversity has touched me") and **affirmed Allah's attribute of mercy** ("You are the Most Merciful") — leaving the request *implied* and the decision to Allah. It is the prayer of a servant who trusts his Master completely. Allah "responded to him" — teaching that this humble, trusting manner of du'a is beloved and answered. (A model for how to call on Allah in hardship: state your need with humility, affirm His mercy, and trust Him.)

### 3.4 The Healing and the Doubling

> ✅ **Surah Sad (38:42-43)**
>
> **Translation:** "[Allah said,] 'Strike [the ground] with your foot; this is a [spring] for washing, cool and a drink.' And We granted him his family and a like number with them as mercy from Us and a reminder for those of understanding."
>
> **Tafsir:** Allah's relief came through a simple command — strike the ground with his foot — producing a **healing spring** to wash in and drink from, restoring his health. Then Allah **restored his family and doubled it** ("his family and a like number with them"). The trial ended not just in restoration but in *increase* — a recurring divine pattern: **patience through loss is rewarded with more than was lost** (as with Yusuf, and as the Prophet ﷺ taught about reward for the patient: "their reward [is] without account" — 39:10). Ayyub became "a reminder for those of understanding."

### 3.5 The Gentle Resolution of His Oath

> ✅ **Surah Sad (38:44):** "[And We said,] 'Take in your hand a bunch [of grass/twigs] and strike with it, and do not break your oath.'"
>
> **Tafsir:** A tender detail: tradition holds that during his trial, Ayyub had sworn an oath (in a moment of distress) to strike someone (often said to be his wife, over a misunderstanding) a certain number of lashes. After his healing, not wishing his faithful wife to be harmed yet not wishing to break a vow to Allah, Allah gave him a **merciful solution**: take a bundle of many thin twigs/grass and strike *once* with the bundle, fulfilling the number gently. **Lesson:** Allah's law combines **keeping one's word to Him** with **mercy and kindness** — a beautiful accommodation honouring both the oath and the loyal wife. (This became a basis in Islamic law for certain accommodations in fulfilling oaths.)

Allah called, "O Ayyub, did I not make you rich enough to need what you see?" He said, "Yes, my Lord, but I cannot do without Your blessing." A beautiful glimpse: even restored and wealthy, Ayyub's heart clung to *every* blessing of Allah with gratitude, never taking abundance for granted.

## 5. The Themes

1. **Trials are tests, not punishments — and the best are tested most.** Ayyub's suffering raised his rank; "the most severely tested are the prophets" (Tirmidhi 2398). Hardship can be a sign of Allah's love and a means of elevation and expiation.
2. **Patience (*sabr*) is active, not passive.** Ayyub's *sabr* was steadfast faith + continued worship + hope — "ever-returning to Allah" (38:44). Patience is holding firm *with* Allah, not mere endurance.
3. **There is *adab* (etiquette) in calling upon Allah.** Ayyub stated his need gently and affirmed Allah's mercy, without demand or complaint (21:83). This trusting humility is the beloved manner of du'a.
4. **Patience is rewarded with more than was lost.** Allah restored Ayyub's family *and doubled it* (38:43). "The patient will be given their reward without account" (39:10).
5. **Gratitude in abundance, patience in loss.** Ayyub thanked Allah in wealth (the locusts) and trusted Him in loss — the believer's two wings.
6. **Allah's law unites faithfulness and mercy** (the oath and the twigs, 38:44).

---

## 6. Lessons

1. **When tested, don't lose faith — and don't lose your manners with Allah.** Ayyub never complained against Allah; his du'a was gentle and trusting. In your hardship, state your need humbly, affirm Allah's mercy, and trust His timing.
2. **Suffering is not abandonment.** The most beloved servants are often the most tested. Your trial may be raising your rank and erasing your sins (Bukhari 5645).
3. **Patience is the path, and it pays.** What you lose with patience, Allah can restore manifold. Hold on.
4. **Never despair of Allah's mercy.** Even after years of affliction, relief came in an instant by Allah's command. Relief is always near for the patient.
5. **Stay grateful even when restored.** Like Ayyub gathering the golden locusts, cherish every blessing and never grow heedless in ease.

## 7. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"Ayyub's suffering meant Allah was angry with him / he was being punished."**
> ✅ The opposite — it was the **trial of a beloved prophet**, raising his rank ("the most tested are the prophets," Tirmidhi 2398). Trials are not proof of divine displeasure.

> ❌ **"Ayyub complained to Allah about his suffering."**
> ✅ He did **not** complain — his du'a (21:83) was a model of *adab*, gently stating his condition and affirming Allah's mercy, never objecting.

> ❌ **"The gory Isra'iliyyat details (worms eating his flesh, his body rotting, being thrown on a dunghill) are part of the Islamic story."**
> ✅ The Qur'an **deliberately omits** these; they are **Isra'iliyyat** (§8). Some even contradict the dignity Allah grants His prophets. Focus on the *patience* and *restoration*, as the Qur'an does.

> ❌ **"Patience means passively giving up."**
> ✅ Ayyub's patience was **active** — continued faith, worship, and du'a (he was *awwab*, "ever-returning"). *Sabr* is steadfastness with Allah, not resignation.

## 8. Weak / Isra'iliyyat Narrations (Labelled)

---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 1 — The gruesome details of his illness**

- **Narration:** That worms ate his flesh, his body decayed and stank, he was cast out of his town onto a rubbish heap, all his skin fell off, etc.
- **Source:** **Isra'iliyyat** absorbed into some tafsir; the Qur'an mentions only "adversity/affliction" (*durr*) without these details.
- **Why questionable:** Many scholars hold these contradict the dignity Allah preserves for His prophets (whose afflictions do not include repulsive disfigurement that would drive people from their message). 
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ **NO** for the gory specifics. The Qur'an's restraint is deliberate — focus on *sabr*.
---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 2 — The wife's name and the oath details**

- **Narration:** Naming his wife (e.g., "Rahma"), and elaborate accounts of the oath to strike her and Shaytan's deception of her.
- **Source:** Tradition; the Qur'an gives the *oath resolution* (38:44) without the wife's name or full backstory.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ The oath-and-twigs solution is Qur'anic; the names and detailed backstory are unverified.

## 12. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: What did Ayyub lose?**
A: His **wealth, his children, and his health** — virtually everything — over a prolonged trial, with (by tradition) only his faithful wife remaining.

**Q: How long did he suffer?**
A: The Qur'an doesn't specify; tradition mentions many years (figures vary, unverified). The point is the *length and severity* that made his patience legendary.

**Q: Did Ayyub ever complain to Allah?**
A: No — his du'a (21:83) was a model of patience and *adab*: gently stating his condition and affirming Allah's mercy, without objection or demand.

**Q: How was he healed?**
A: Allah told him to strike the ground with his foot; a **healing spring** gushed forth to wash in and drink, restoring his health (38:42).

**Q: What's the deal with the bundle of twigs?**
A: Ayyub had sworn an oath (in distress) to strike someone (traditionally his wife); Allah mercifully let him fulfil it by striking *once* with a bundle of many thin twigs — honouring the oath without harming his loyal wife (38:44).

**Q: Are the gory details (worms, rotting) true?**
A: **No** — they're Isra'iliyyat the Qur'an deliberately omits (§8). Focus on his patience.
