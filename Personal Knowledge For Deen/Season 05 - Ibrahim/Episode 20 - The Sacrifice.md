# Season 05 · Episode 20
# The Sacrifice
### The Ultimate Test — A Father, a Son, and Total Submission

> *"O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, of the patient."*
> — **Qur'an, Surah As-Saffat (37:102)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

This is the climax of Ibrahim's life and one of the most profound stories in all of revelation: Allah commanded Ibrahim, in a dream, to **sacrifice his own beloved son** — and both father and son **submitted**. This chapter covers the long-awaited birth of Isma'il, the trial of leaving Hajar and the baby in the desert valley of Makkah (the origin of Zamzam and the Hajj rite of Sa'i), and the supreme test of the sacrifice — the event commemorated by Muslims worldwide every year on **'Eid al-Adha**. It is the definitive lesson on what *Islam* (submission) truly means.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

After years of longing, Ibrahim — now elderly — was blessed with a son, **Isma'il (Ishmael)**, born to Hajar. But the trials of submission had only begun. By Allah's command, Ibrahim left Hajar and the infant Isma'il in the **barren valley of Bakkah (Makkah)** — no water, no people, no provisions. Hajar's response is immortalized: when told it was Allah's command, she said, *"Then Allah will not neglect us."* Her desperate running between the hills of **Safa and Marwah** searching for water, and the miraculous gushing of the **well of Zamzam** at the baby's feet, became permanent rites of the Hajj performed by millions to this day.

Years later, when Isma'il had grown to the age of working alongside his father, Ibrahim received the ultimate command — in a **dream**: *to sacrifice his son.* A prophet's true dream is revelation. Ibrahim, with breaking heart but total submission, told his son. And in one of the most beautiful moments in scripture, the **son submitted too**: *"O my father, do as you are commanded; you will find me, if Allah wills, of the patient."* As Ibrahim laid him down and prepared to fulfil the command, Allah called out: *"O Ibrahim, you have fulfilled the vision!"* — and **ransomed the son with a great sacrifice** (a ram). The command was never about the killing; it was about the *submission* — and both passed the test completely.

This event is commemorated annually by Muslims worldwide in the **sacrifice of 'Eid al-Adha**, and it cements Ibrahim and Isma'il as the supreme models of submission to Allah.

## 3. The Story: Part One — The Desert Valley (Hajar & Zamzam)

### 3.1 The Long-Awaited Son

> ✅ **Surah As-Saffat (37:100-101):** "[Ibrahim prayed,] 'My Lord, grant me [a child] from among the righteous.' So We gave him good tidings of a forbearing boy."
>
> **Tafsir:** Ibrahim, having left his homeland and family for Allah, longed for righteous offspring to carry the mission. **Isma'il** was the answer — born to **Hajar**, the Egyptian woman who had come into their household (Episode 18). (Ishaq was born later to Sarah — Episodes 18-19.) The "forbearing boy" (*ghulam halim*) is Isma'il, whose forbearance would soon be tested beyond imagination.

### 3.2 Left in the Valley — "Allah Will Not Neglect Us"

> ✅ **Sahih al-Bukhari 3364** — *Authentic.* Ibn 'Abbas narrated the story in detail: Ibrahim brought Hajar and the nursing Isma'il to the barren valley of Makkah (where the Ka'bah would later stand), left them with a little water and dates, and turned to leave. Hajar followed, asking, "To whom are you leaving us in this valley with no one and nothing?" He did not answer. She asked, **"Has Allah commanded you to do this?"** He said, "Yes." She replied, **"Then He will not neglect us."**
>
> **Explanation:** Hajar's words are among the greatest expressions of *tawakkul* (reliance on Allah) by any human being. A woman left alone with a baby in a lifeless desert — and her response was not despair but **certainty in Allah.** Her faith was rewarded in a way that echoes through history.

### 3.3 The Running and the Well of Zamzam

> ✅ **Sahih al-Bukhari 3364 (cont.):** When the water ran out and the baby cried with thirst, Hajar **ran between the two hills of Safa and Marwah seven times**, searching desperately for help or water. Then the angel (Jibril) struck the ground (by the baby's feet) and **water gushed forth — Zamzam.** Hajar contained it, saying *"Zam, zam"* (gather/stop). The Prophet ﷺ said: *"May Allah have mercy on the mother of Isma'il! Had she left Zamzam [flowing freely], it would have been a flowing stream."* Later, a passing Arab tribe, **Jurhum**, saw birds circling (a sign of water), settled there with Hajar's permission, and the place came to life. Isma'il grew up among them and learned Arabic.
>
> **Explanation & legacy:** Hajar's running became the Hajj rite of **Sa'i** — every pilgrim, for thousands of years, re-enacts a single mother's desperate search for water, honouring her faith. **Zamzam** still flows today beside the Ka'bah. From this "neglected" family in a dead valley, Allah raised the sacred city of Makkah and the lineage that would produce the final Prophet ﷺ. *Hajar's "Allah will not neglect us" was answered for all time.*

## 4. The Story: Part Two — The Sacrifice

### 4.1 The Command in the Dream

> ✅ **Surah As-Saffat (37:102)**
>
> **Arabic:** فَلَمَّا بَلَغَ مَعَهُ السَّعْيَ قَالَ يَا بُنَيَّ إِنِّي أَرَىٰ فِي الْمَنَامِ أَنِّي أَذْبَحُكَ فَانظُرْ مَاذَا تَرَىٰ ۚ قَالَ يَا أَبَتِ افْعَلْ مَا تُؤْمَرُ ۖ سَتَجِدُنِي إِن شَاءَ اللَّهُ مِنَ الصَّابِرِينَ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Falamma balagha ma'ahu as-sa'ya qala ya bunayya inni ara fi al-manami anni adhbahuka fanzur madha tara. Qala ya abati if'al ma tu'mar, satajiduni in sha'Allahu mina as-sabirin.*
>
> **Translation:** "And when he reached with him [the age of] working/striving, [Ibrahim] said, 'O my son, indeed I have seen in a dream that I [must] sacrifice you, so see what you think.' He said, 'O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, of the patient.'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** Several profound points:
> - **The dreams of prophets are revelation** — so this vision was a true command from Allah, not an ordinary dream.
> - Ibrahim **consulted his son** ("see what you think") — not because the command was negotiable, but to involve Isma'il in the submission, making it *his* test too.
> - **The son's response is breathtaking:** no fear, no protest, no plea — only *"do as you are commanded; you will find me patient, Allah willing."* He even adds "*in sha'Allah*," seeking Allah's help to be steadfast. This is a *teenage boy* calmly submitting to his own sacrifice out of love for Allah. Both father and son reached the summit of *islam* (submission).

### 4.2 The Submission and the Ransom

> ✅ **Surah As-Saffat (37:103-107)**
>
> **Translation:** "And when they had both submitted, and he put him down upon his forehead, We called to him, 'O Ibrahim! You have fulfilled the vision.' Indeed, We thus reward the doers of good. Indeed, this was the clear trial. And We ransomed him with a great sacrifice."
>
> **Tafsir:** The key phrase is **"when they had both submitted" (*aslama*)** — the root of the word *Islam* itself. The moment their submission was *complete* and *proven*, the purpose of the test was achieved — and Allah stopped the act. **The command was never about Ibrahim killing his son; it was about whether he would submit even his most beloved to Allah.** Having passed, Allah **ransomed** the boy with "a great sacrifice" (a magnificent ram), and declared Ibrahim among the *muhsinin* (those who do good with excellence). The son was saved, the father honoured, and the lesson sealed: *true love of Allah surrenders everything to Him — and Allah, in return, gives back what was surrendered, multiplied in blessing.*

### 4.3 The Legacy: 'Eid al-Adha

This event is the origin of the **Sacrifice ('Udhiyah)** that Muslims perform every year during **'Eid al-Adha** (the Festival of Sacrifice), at the climax of the Hajj. Around the world, Muslims slaughter livestock (sheep, goats, cattle, camels) and distribute the meat to family, neighbours, and the poor — re-living Ibrahim's submission and Allah's mercy. It is one of the two great festivals of Islam, observed by over a billion people, all tracing back to this single act of faith.

## 5. Which Son Was It? (Scholarly Discussion)

A well-known question: was the son to be sacrificed **Isma'il** or **Ishaq**?

- **Opinion 1 — Isma'il (the firstborn).** *Evidence:* The Qur'anic sequence in Surah As-Saffat mentions the sacrifice (37:102-107) and **then** gives the glad tidings of **Ishaq** (37:112) — implying the son in the sacrifice was the *earlier* son, Isma'il. Also, Isma'il was the only son in Makkah with Ibrahim. *Held by:* the **majority of Muslim scholars** (and the dominant view in Islam), including most later verifiers.
- **Opinion 2 — Ishaq.** *Held by:* some early scholars (influenced partly by reports from the People of the Book, where the Biblical text names Isaac). *Strength:* weaker by the Qur'anic context.

📖 **Conclusion:** The **strongest and majority view is Isma'il**, supported by the structure of Surah As-Saffat (the sacrifice account precedes the separate glad tidings of Ishaq) and by Isma'il's presence with Ibrahim in Makkah. *(This is also why the rites connected to the sacrifice are tied to Makkah and Mina, the land of Isma'il.)*

## 7. Lessons

1. **Islam means submission — total, even of what you love most.** "When they had both submitted..." (37:103). The test was the willingness to surrender the *most beloved* thing to Allah. Faith is proven not in easy times but when Allah asks for what is dearest.
2. **What you surrender to Allah, He returns multiplied.** Ibrahim was ready to give up his son — and got his son back, plus an eternal legacy, a great festival, and the honour of all generations. Allah never takes from a sincere servant without giving better.
3. **"Allah will not neglect us."** Hajar's *tawakkul* in a dead valley birthed a sacred city, a sacred well, and a sacred rite. Place your trust in Allah in your hardest moment, and watch what He builds from it.
4. **True dreams and divine commands override personal desire.** Ibrahim's love for his son was immense — yet his love for Allah was greater. The hierarchy of love defines the believer.
5. **Righteous children are a gift and a test.** Isma'il's submission shows the fruit of righteous upbringing: a child who loves Allah enough to submit alongside his father. Raising such children is part of the legacy.
6. **The rites of Hajj are living history.** Every Sa'i, every sacrifice, every pilgrim's step re-enacts the faith of Ibrahim and Hajar — keeping submission alive across millennia.

## 8. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"Allah actually wanted a human sacrifice / nearly made Ibrahim a murderer."**
> ✅ The command's purpose was to test **submission**, not to take the boy's life. Allah stopped it the instant the submission was proven and **ransomed** the son (37:107). Islam utterly forbids human sacrifice; this event in fact *ended* with a ram, modeling the *replacement* of any such notion.

> ❌ **"The son resisted / had to be tricked."**
> ✅ The son **willingly submitted**: "do as you are commanded; you will find me patient" (37:102). It was a shared act of faith, not a deception.

> ❌ **"It was definitely Ishaq (as in the Bible)."**
> ✅ The **majority Islamic view is Isma'il**, supported by the Qur'anic context (§5). This is a point where Islam differs from the Biblical text.

> ❌ **"'Eid al-Adha is about celebrating killing animals."**
> ✅ It commemorates **Ibrahim's submission and Allah's mercy**; the sacrifice symbolizes surrender to Allah, and its meat feeds family and the poor — "Their meat will not reach Allah, nor their blood, but what reaches Him is your piety (*taqwa*)" (22:37).

## 9. Weak / Isra'iliyyat Narrations (Labelled)

---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 1 — Shaytan's interference and the origin of stoning the Jamarat**

- **Narration:** That as Ibrahim went to fulfil the command, Shaytan appeared three times to dissuade him (and Hajar/Isma'il), and Ibrahim pelted him with stones at three spots — said to be the origin of the **stoning of the Jamarat** at Mina during Hajj.
- **Source:** This is **widely related in tradition** and connected to the Hajj rite, though its chains are debated; some scholars accept the *connection* of the rite to Ibrahim's defiance of Shaytan as established by practice, while the detailed dialogue is Isra'iliyyat-influenced.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ The *rite* of stoning the Jamarat is part of Hajj by the Prophet's ﷺ Sunnah; the *detailed Shaytan-dialogue backstory* is traditional and not firmly authenticated. Treat the backstory as illustrative, not certain.
---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 2 — Exact ages, the knife "refusing to cut," detailed dialogues**

- **Source:** Tradition/storyteller embellishment.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ The Qur'an's account is complete; these details are unverified.

## 10. Important People

**Ibrahim (peace be upon him):** The supreme model of submission — willing to sacrifice his own son for Allah. Rewarded with an everlasting legacy.

**Isma'il (Ishmael), peace be upon him:** The "forbearing boy," who willingly submitted to his own sacrifice — a prophet, ancestor of the Arabs and of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and co-builder of the Ka'bah (Episode 21).

**Hajar (Hagar):** Mother of Isma'il; her *tawakkul* ("Allah will not neglect us") and her running between Safa and Marwah are honoured forever in the Hajj. One of the great women of faith.

## 13. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Which son was Ibrahim told to sacrifice?**
A: The **majority view is Isma'il**, based on the Qur'anic context (the glad tidings of Ishaq come *after* the sacrifice account, 37:112). A minority said Ishaq.

**Q: Did Ibrahim actually have to kill his son?**
A: No — the test was his *willingness to submit*. The moment he proved it, Allah stopped him and ransomed the boy with a ram (37:107). Human sacrifice is forbidden in Islam.

**Q: Why do Muslims sacrifice animals on 'Eid al-Adha?**
A: To commemorate Ibrahim's submission and Allah's mercy; it symbolizes surrender to Allah, and the meat feeds family, neighbours, and the poor (22:37).

**Q: How is the well of Zamzam connected to this story?**
A: It gushed forth to save the infant Isma'il and Hajar in the barren valley (Bukhari 3364); it still flows today and pilgrims drink from it.

**Q: Is the story of Shaytan and the stoning of the Jamarat authentic?**
A: The *rite* of stoning is established by the Prophet's ﷺ Hajj; the detailed backstory of Shaytan tempting Ibrahim is traditional and not firmly authenticated (§9).
