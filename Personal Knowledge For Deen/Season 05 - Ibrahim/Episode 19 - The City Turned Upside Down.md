# Season 05 · Episode 19
# The City Turned Upside Down
### Lut, the People of Sodom, and a Nation's Self-Destruction

> *"And [We sent] Lut, when he said to his people, 'Do you commit immorality such as no one in the worlds had committed before you?'"*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Al-A'raf (7:80)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

Ibrahim migrated toward the blessed land with his nephew **Lut (Lot), peace be upon him**, who was himself made a prophet and sent to the cities of the plain (traditionally **Sodom**, near the Dead Sea). This chapter covers Lut's mission to a society that had institutionalized a grave immorality and injustice, his patient warning, the arrival of the angels (who also brought Ibrahim glad tidings), and the catastrophic destruction of the cities. It is told here, as in the Qur'an, with **moral seriousness and dignity** — focused on the lessons of divine justice, the duty of the believer in a corrupt society, and the mercy that saved Lut and his family.

> 📖 **Tone note:** This chapter discusses the sin of Lut's people at the level the Qur'an does — naming the wrong and its judgement — without graphic detail. The aim is understanding the *theological and historical* lesson, in keeping with this encyclopedia's serious, respectful approach.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

Lut was the nephew (brother's son) of Ibrahim, among the first to believe in him (29:26), and a prophet in his own right. He was sent to the people of the cities of the plain — wealthy, settled communities that had fallen into multiple grave sins: most distinctively, they were the **first people in history to openly practise and normalize homosexual acts** (the Qur'an stresses "such as no one had committed before you" — 7:80), alongside **highway robbery, public indecency, and the abuse of travellers/guests**. Their society had inverted the natural moral order, and they did so openly and defiantly.

Lut called them, with patience and clarity, to fear Allah, abandon their transgression, and return to what is pure and natural. Their response was not argument but **rejection and threats** — they mocked him and threatened to **expel him and his family** from the town for being "people who keep themselves pure" (27:56), turning virtue itself into a crime.

When the people were beyond reform, Allah sent **angels in the form of handsome young men** — first to Ibrahim (where they gave glad tidings of a son, Ishaq, and informed Ibrahim of the coming destruction, prompting his compassionate pleading), then to Lut. The depravity of the people was proven one last time when they came demanding Lut's "guests." The angels revealed themselves, reassured Lut, and instructed him to leave with his believing family by night — **except his wife**, who had betrayed him. At dawn, the cities were **overturned (turned upside down)** and pelted with **stones of baked clay** — utterly destroyed, leaving ruins as a sign. This chapter explores the mission, the moral diagnosis, the angels' visit, and the judgement.

## 3. Background: Lut and His People

**Lut (Lot)** was the son of Ibrahim's brother (Haran), making him Ibrahim's nephew. He believed in Ibrahim and migrated with him (29:26), then was sent by Allah as a prophet to the cities of the plain — traditionally identified as **Sodom and the surrounding towns** in the region near the **Dead Sea** (the lowest-lying region on earth, between modern Jordan and Palestine). The Qur'an calls the destroyed settlement *al-Mu'tafikah* ("the overturned") and notes its ruins lay "on an established road" that the later Arabs passed (15:76; 37:137-138).

These were prosperous, settled communities — but morally bankrupt. The Qur'an specifies their sins:

> ✅ **Surah Al-A'raf (7:80-81):** "Do you commit immorality such as no one in the worlds had committed before you? Indeed, you approach men with desire instead of women. Rather, you are a transgressing people."
> ✅ **Surah Al-'Ankabut (29:29):** "Indeed, you approach men and you obstruct the road (commit highway robbery) and commit in your meetings (every) evil." — *Their sins were plural: sexual transgression, banditry, and public depravity.*

## 4. The Story

### 4.1 Lut's Warning

> ✅ **Surah Ash-Shu'ara (26:160-166)**
>
> **Translation:** "The people of Lut denied the messengers, when their brother Lut said to them, 'Will you not fear Allah? Indeed, I am to you a trustworthy messenger. So fear Allah and obey me. And I do not ask you for it any payment... Do you approach males among the worlds and leave what your Lord has created for you as mates? Rather, you are a transgressing people.'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** Lut's call follows the universal prophetic pattern (fear Allah, obey the messenger, I seek no payment), with a specific focus on their defining sin. He appeals to the **natural order** ("what your Lord created for you as mates") — the wrongness lies in inverting the *fitrah* Allah established. His tone is that of a sincere reformer pleading his people back to purity.

### 4.2 Turning Virtue Into a Crime

> ✅ **Surah An-Naml (27:56)**
>
> **Translation:** "But the answer of his people was not except that they said, 'Expel the family of Lut from your city. Indeed, they are people who keep themselves pure (*yatatahharun*)!'"
>
> **Tafsir:** A chilling sign of a thoroughly corrupted society: they mocked Lut's family for **"keeping themselves pure"** — turning *chastity and decency into the offense.* When a community sinks far enough, it doesn't just commit wrong; it *inverts values* — celebrating vice and persecuting virtue. This is one of the gravest stages of moral collapse the Qur'an describes.

### 4.3 The Angels Visit Ibrahim — Glad Tidings and Warning

Before reaching Lut, the angels stopped at Ibrahim's:

> ✅ **Surah Hud (11:69-76)** & **Surah Adh-Dhariyat (51:24-34):** The angels (as guests) declined Ibrahim's roasted calf (revealing they were not human — Episode 02), then gave him **glad tidings of a knowledgeable son (Ishaq), and after him Ya'qub.** Ibrahim's aged wife Sarah laughed in astonishment ("Shall I give birth when I am an old woman?"). The angels then told Ibrahim they were sent to **destroy Lut's people.** Ibrahim — *"forbearing, compassionate, and oft-returning to Allah"* (11:75) — began to **plead and argue** for them, until the angels said: "O Ibrahim, give up this; the command of your Lord has come, and there will reach them an irreversible punishment" (11:76).
>
> **Tafsir:** This reveals Ibrahim's beautiful character: even toward a doomed, sinful people, his instinct was *mercy* — he pleaded for them. It also shows the limit of intercession: once Allah's just decree is final, even a beloved prophet's pleading cannot reverse it. (And note: the destruction of Lut's people and the *birth of Ishaq* are intertwined in the same visit — judgement on the corrupt, renewal through the righteous.)

### 4.4 The Angels Reach Lut — and the People Expose Themselves

> ✅ **Surah Hud (11:77-81)**
>
> **Translation:** "And when Our messengers came to Lut, he was distressed for them and felt for them great discomfort and said, 'This is a trying day.' And his people came hastening to him, and before this they had been doing evil deeds. He said, 'O my people, these are my daughters; they are purer for you (in lawful marriage)... so fear Allah and do not disgrace me concerning my guests...'... [The angels] said, 'O Lut, indeed we are messengers of your Lord; they will never reach you...'"
>
> **Tafsir:** Lut, not knowing they were angels, was **terrified for his guests** — knowing his people's predatory habits toward male travellers. When the mob descended demanding the "guests," Lut, in desperation, offered the lawful path (marriage to the women of the community — "my daughters" understood by many as the women of his people, whom a prophet is a "father" to, urging *lawful* relations). The people refused, proving their sin was not lack of options but deliberate transgression. At this, the **angels revealed their identity**, reassured Lut, and (by one account) struck the aggressors blind (54:37). The people's depravity was now fully documented before the judgement.

### 4.5 The Destruction

> ✅ **Surah Hud (11:81-83)**
>
> **Translation:** "[The angels said,] 'Set out with your family during a portion of the night and let not any among you look back — except your wife; indeed, she will be struck by that which strikes them...' So when Our command came, We made the highest part [of the cities] the lowest and rained upon them stones of layered hard clay, marked from your Lord. And it (this punishment) is not far from the wrongdoers."
>
> **Tafsir:** The punishment was twofold and overwhelming:
> 1. **The cities were overturned** — "We made their highest the lowest" — flipped upside down (hence *al-Mu'tafikah*, "the overturned"). Many scholars link this to a violent geological upheaval in the Dead Sea region.
> 2. **Stones of baked clay** rained down, "marked" — each destined for its target.
>
> Lut and his believing family escaped by night — **except his wife**, who had sided with the people and was destroyed with them (66:10). The lesson of *faith over family* returns: a prophet's own wife perished by her own disbelief, exactly as Nuh's wife did (Episode 12).

## 6. The Lessons — Moral and Theological

This story is preserved by Allah as a warning, and its lessons are drawn at the level of principle:

1. **Allah established a natural order, and societies that invert it invite ruin.** The Qur'an roots the wrongness of the people of Lut in their abandoning "what Allah created for you as mates" (26:165-166) — a transgression against the *fitrah* and the divine design.
2. **The gravest stage of corruption is when society celebrates vice and persecutes virtue.** "Expel them — they keep themselves pure!" (27:56). When good becomes the crime, judgement is near.
3. **A believer must not be silenced by a hostile majority.** Lut stood alone for purity against an entire society that mocked and threatened him. Numbers never determine truth.
4. **Mercy pleads, but justice is real.** Ibrahim's compassionate pleading shows the believer's heart inclines to mercy; the angels' firm reply shows that Allah's just decree, once final, is irreversible.
5. **Faith, not family, saves — again.** Lut's wife perished despite being a prophet's spouse; his believing family was saved. Salvation is personal (66:10).
6. **Sin is to be hated; the duty is to call to good.** The Islamic stance, modeled by Lut, is to call people *away* from transgression toward purity and the lawful — with clarity *and* with the sincere desire for their guidance and salvation, not with cruelty.

## 7. Weak / Isra'iliyyat Narrations (Labelled)

---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 1 — Graphic and elaborate details**

- **Narration:** Extensive lurid descriptions of the people's acts, the exact number of cities and inhabitants, named individuals, and the precise manner of the overturning (e.g., Jibril lifting the cities on his wing to the heavens).
- **Source:** Isra'iliyyat and storyteller tradition.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ The Qur'anic account (their sins, the warning, the overturning, the stones) is complete and sufficient. The elaborate staging is unverified; and graphic detail serves no benefit.
---
**📖 Clarification 2 — "My daughters" (11:78)**

- Scholars differ on whether Lut literally offered his biological daughters (in lawful marriage) or meant *the women of his community* (a prophet being like a "father" to his people, urging them toward *lawful* relations). The **majority** lean toward the latter (the women of the town, via marriage). Either way, the point is that Lut directed them to the **lawful and pure** alternative, which they refused.

## 11. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"The people of Lut were destroyed only for inhospitality, not for the sexual sin."**
> ✅ The Qur'an explicitly names their sexual transgression as central ("such as no one had committed before you... you approach men," 7:80-81; 26:165-166), *alongside* banditry and public evil (29:29). It was a combination, with the unprecedented immorality foremost.

> ❌ **"Lut's relation to Ibrahim makes the story legendary."**
> ✅ Lut was Ibrahim's nephew and a genuine prophet (29:26; 6:86); the Qur'an presents both as real historical figures, and the ruins as a real, visited site.

> ❌ **"A prophet's family is automatically saved."**
> ✅ Lut's **wife perished** (66:10) — saved are those with *faith*, not those merely related to the righteous.

> ❌ **"The story teaches cruelty toward sinners."**
> ✅ It teaches *calling people away from sin toward purity* (as Lut did) and the reality of divine justice for *defiant, society-wide, unrepentant* transgression after warning. Ibrahim's pleading mercy is part of the very same account.

## 12. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Who was Lut in relation to Ibrahim?**
A: His nephew (brother's son) and an early believer who migrated with him (29:26); later a prophet to the cities of the plain.

**Q: Where were the cities?**
A: Traditionally **Sodom** and neighbouring towns near the **Dead Sea** — *al-Mu'tafikah*, "the overturned," whose ruins lay on a known road (15:76).

**Q: Why did the angels come to Ibrahim first?**
A: To deliver glad tidings of his son **Ishaq** and to inform him of the coming judgement on Lut's people — joining renewal (a righteous son) and justice (a corrupt nation) in one visit.

**Q: What happened to Lut's wife?**
A: She betrayed him by siding with the people and was destroyed with them (66:10) — a lesson that faith, not marriage to a prophet, determines one's fate.

**Q: How were the cities destroyed?**
A: They were **overturned** ("highest made lowest") and **rained with stones of baked clay** at dawn (11:82-83).
