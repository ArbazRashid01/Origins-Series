# Season 05 · Episode 16
# The Boy Who Questioned Everything
### Ibrahim's Search for the One True God

> *"So when the night covered him [with darkness], he saw a star. He said, 'This is my lord.' But when it set, he said, 'I do not love things that set.'"*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Al-An'am (6:76)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

We now arrive at one of the towering figures of all human history: **Ibrahim (Abraham), peace be upon him** — *Khalil-Allah*, "the intimate friend of Allah." More verses, prophets, and nations flow from him than from almost anyone else in the Qur'an. This first chapter of his season is about his **mind and heart**: how a young man, surrounded by idol-worship and star-worship, used pure reasoning and a sound *fitrah* to find his way to the One God — and then began the confrontation that would define his life.

> 📖 **Method note:** Ibrahim's story is spread across many surahs (Al-An'am 6, Maryam 19, Al-Anbiya 21, Ash-Shu'ara 26, As-Saffat 37, Ibrahim 14, and others). We build from these and authentic Sunnah. His father's name in the Qur'an is **Azar** (6:74); the relationship between "Azar" and the Biblical "Terah" is discussed in §9.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

Ibrahim was born into a society sunk in **idolatry and astral worship** — likely in **Mesopotamia** (the region of ancient Babylon / Ur, southern Iraq), ruled by a tyrant (traditionally Nimrod) and served by his own father **Azar**, who was an idol-maker or idol-priest. From a young age, Ibrahim's sound nature recoiled from worshipping carved stones that could neither hear, see, nor help.

The Qur'an gives us a remarkable window into his *reasoning*: observing the heavens, he considered a **star**, then the **moon**, then the **sun** — each time noting that a true Lord cannot be something that *rises and sets*, that comes and goes. Through this process of reflection (which the Qur'an presents as a teaching method, not genuine doubt on his part), he declared his complete rejection of all created "gods" and turned his face purely to **the One who created the heavens and the earth.**

He then began, gently and rationally, to call his own father and his people away from idols — using questions that exposed the absurdity of worshipping objects one carves with one's own hands. This chapter covers his intellectual journey to *tawhid*, his respectful but firm appeal to his father, and the establishment of Ibrahim as the great rational monotheist — setting up his dramatic confrontation with the idols and the king (Episodes 17-18).

## 3. Background: A World of Idols and Stars

Ibrahim's world (traditionally **Ur / Babylon** in southern Mesopotamia, c. 2000-1800 BCE by common reckoning — dates uncertain) was a sophisticated civilization steeped in **polytheism**. People worshipped:
- **Carved idols** of wood and stone, kept in homes and temples;
- **Celestial bodies** — the sun, moon, planets, and stars — associated with deities (astral religion was central to Mesopotamian/Babylonian culture);
- A **divine-king** cult, where the ruler (traditionally **Nimrod**) claimed god-like authority.

Ibrahim's own father, **Azar**, was deeply embedded in this system — by tradition, a craftsman who *made and sold idols*. So Ibrahim's monotheism was not a comfortable inheritance; it was a **revolution against his family, his society, and his state** — making his story a profound study in standing alone for truth.

> ✅ **Surah Al-An'am (6:74):** "And [mention] when Ibrahim said to his father Azar, 'Do you take idols as deities? Indeed, I see you and your people in clear error.'"

## 4. The Story: The Search for the Lord

### 4.1 A Sound Nature Rejecting the Absurd

Even before the famous star-reflection, Ibrahim's *fitrah* (innate disposition toward truth) made him question the absurdity around him:

> ✅ **Surah Al-Anbiya (21:52)**
>
> **Translation:** "When he said to his father and his people, 'What are these statues to which you are devoted?'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir):** A simple, devastating question from a young man: *what are these things you bow to?* Ibrahim saw what his elders, blinded by tradition, could not — that worshipping objects shaped by human hands made no sense. His was the clarity of an uncorrupted *fitrah* refusing to accept "because our fathers did" (21:53-54) as a reason for anything.

### 4.2 The Reflection on Star, Moon, and Sun

This is one of the most famous passages of reasoning in the Qur'an:

> ✅ **Surah Al-An'am (6:76-79)**
>
> **Arabic:** فَلَمَّا جَنَّ عَلَيْهِ اللَّيْلُ رَأَىٰ كَوْكَبًا ۖ قَالَ هَٰذَا رَبِّي ۖ فَلَمَّا أَفَلَ قَالَ لَا أُحِبُّ الْآفِلِينَ...
>
> **Translation:** "So when the night covered him, he saw a star. He said, 'This is my lord.' But when it set, he said, 'I do not love things that set.' And when he saw the moon rising, he said, 'This is my lord.' But when it set, he said, 'Unless my Lord guides me, I will surely be among the people gone astray.' And when he saw the sun rising, he said, 'This is my lord; this is greater.' But when it set, he said, 'O my people, indeed I am free from what you associate with Allah. Indeed, I have turned my face toward He who created the heavens and the earth, inclining toward truth (*hanifan*), and I am not of those who associate others with Allah.'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir, as-Sa'di):** A crucial point of belief: Ibrahim was **not** genuinely confused about who his Lord was — prophets do not fall into idolatry, and the Qur'an says Allah had already shown him "the kingdom of the heavens and the earth" (6:75). Rather, this was a **teaching demonstration** — Ibrahim *reasoned aloud with his star-worshipping people*, taking up their own premises ("suppose this star is the lord...") and then dismantling them with the obvious flaw: **a true Lord does not disappear, set, or change.** Anything that rises and sets is *governed* — it cannot be the Governor. Step by step, he led them from the small (star) to the great (sun), and showed all of them inadequate, concluding with pure *tawhid*: "I have turned my face to the Creator of the heavens and the earth." This is the Qur'an's model of **faith grounded in reason and reflection.**

### 4.3 The Gentle Appeal to His Father

Ibrahim's call to his idol-priest father is a masterclass in respectful *da'wah* to a misguided parent:

> ✅ **Surah Maryam (19:42-45)**
>
> **Translation:** "When he said to his father, 'O my father, why do you worship that which does not hear and does not see and will not benefit you at all? O my father, indeed there has come to me of knowledge that which has not come to you, so follow me; I will guide you to an even path. O my father, do not worship Shaytan... O my father, indeed I fear that there will touch you a punishment from the Most Merciful...'"
>
> **Tafsir:** Notice Ibrahim's *adab* (manners): he repeats **"O my father" (*ya abati*)** four times — gentle, loving, respectful — even while telling him he is gravely wrong. He uses **reason** ("does not hear or see"), **humility** ("knowledge has come to me"), **invitation** ("follow me, I will guide you"), and **compassionate warning** ("I fear a punishment will touch you"). This is the Qur'anic template for calling family to the truth: firm on the message, tender with the person. Tragically, Azar responded with **threats** ("if you do not desist, I will surely stone you; leave me for a long time" — 19:46), yet Ibrahim replied only with peace and a promise to seek forgiveness for him (19:47).

## 5. Ibrahim's Rank: Khalil-Allah and the Hanif

Ibrahim holds a unique station in Islam:

- **Khalil-Allah** — "the intimate friend of Allah": *"And Allah took Ibrahim as an intimate friend (khalil)"* (4:125). This is the highest level of love, shared in the texts with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ (who said he too was Allah's *khalil*, Sahih Muslim 532).
- **The Hanif** — the pure monotheist, naturally inclined to truth, neither Jew nor Christian nor idolater: *"Ibrahim was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was one inclining toward truth (hanifan), a Muslim; and he was not of the polytheists"* (3:67).
- **Imam (leader) for mankind** — *"Indeed, I will make you a leader for the people"* (2:124).
- **Father of the prophets** — the prophetic line flows through his sons Isma'il and Ishaq; *"and We placed in his descendants prophethood and the Scripture"* (29:27).
- One of the five ***Ulu al-'Azm*** (the Messengers of firm resolve).
- The Muslim invokes him in **every prayer** (the *Ibrahimi* salutation: "...as You sent blessings upon Ibrahim and the family of Ibrahim").

## 7. Lessons

1. **Faith and reason are allies, not enemies.** Ibrahim reasoned his way toward Allah, reflecting on creation. Islam invites us to *think* — "Do they not reflect upon the heavens and the earth?" (7:185). Blind imitation ("our fathers did it") is precisely what Ibrahim rejected.
2. **A pure heart recognizes its Creator.** The *fitrah* points to the One God; idolatry is a corruption *of* nature, not its expression. When Ibrahim cleared away the noise, the truth was self-evident.
3. **Truth may require standing against your own family and society.** Ibrahim opposed his father, his people, and his king — alone. Conviction sometimes costs you everything familiar.
4. **Call your loved ones with gentleness and reason.** "O my father..." (19:42). Even when family is deeply wrong, the believer combines firmness on truth with tenderness toward the person.
5. **"I do not love things that set."** A profound spiritual principle: do not attach your ultimate love and hope to anything that fades, changes, or dies — wealth, status, beauty, people. Attach it only to the One who never sets.

## 8. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"Ibrahim genuinely thought a star/the moon/the sun was God, then changed his mind."**
> ✅ No — he was **demonstrating** to his people, using their premises to expose their error (Allah had already guided him, 6:75). Prophets do not lapse into idolatry. It was a teaching method.

> ❌ **"Ibrahim was Jewish (or the founder of Judaism/Christianity)."**
> ✅ The Qur'an explicitly says he was *neither* — he was a *hanif*, a pure monotheist who submitted to God (a "muslim" in the linguistic sense) — predating both later traditions (3:67).

> ❌ **"Believing in God requires abandoning the intellect."**
> ✅ Ibrahim's story is the opposite — reflection on creation *leads* to the Creator. Islam treats sound reason as a path to faith.

## 9. Weak / Disputed Points (Labelled)

---
**📖 Clarification 1 — "Azar" vs. "Terah" (the father's name)**

- The Qur'an names Ibrahim's father **Azar** (6:74). The Bible names him **Terah**.
- Scholars offer several reconciliations: "Azar" may have been a second name, a title, an epithet (some say it relates to a word of rebuke or to an idol's name), or the name of an uncle who raised him (a minority view, since the Qur'an clearly uses "father").
- **Verdict:** The Qur'anic "Azar" is authoritative for us. The exact relationship to "Terah" is a historical/linguistic question, not a matter of creed.
---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 2 — Detailed childhood legends (Ibrahim hidden in a cave for years, raised by a gazelle, exact birth year, his mother's name)**

- **Source:** Largely Isra'iliyyat and storyteller tradition.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ Not established. The Qur'an gives us his *reasoning and faith*, not a detailed biography of his infancy.

## 10. Important People

**Ibrahim (peace be upon him):** *Khalil-Allah*, the *hanif*, father of prophets, one of the five *Ulu al-'Azm*. The great rational monotheist. Honoured in the Qur'an above almost all, and invoked in every Muslim's daily prayer.

**Azar (Ibrahim's father):** An idol-maker/priest who rejected his son's call and threatened him; the Qur'an presents Ibrahim's heartbreak over him (and the eventual end of Ibrahim seeking forgiveness for him once it was clear he was an enemy of Allah — 9:114).

**Ibrahim's people & Nimrod (the king):** The idolatrous society and tyrant-king he would soon confront (Episodes 17-18).

---

## 11. Important Places

- **Mesopotamia / Ur / Babylon:** Ibrahim's traditional homeland (southern Iraq), centre of astral religion and idol-worship. (The Qur'an doesn't name the city; tradition and the Bible point to Ur.)
- **The heavens:** His "classroom" — where reflection on the star, moon, and sun led him to the Creator.

---

## 12. Important Objects / Concepts

- **The idols (*asnam / tamathil*):** The carved deities Ibrahim rejected and would soon destroy.
- **The celestial bodies:** Objects of his people's worship and the subjects of his famous reasoning.
- **The *fitrah* and *'aql* (reason):** The two faculties through which Ibrahim found Allah — the heart's natural inclination and the mind's reflection.
- **Hanif:** The term for Ibrahim's pure, upright monotheism.

## 13. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Where and when did Ibrahim live?**
A: Traditionally Ur/Babylon in Mesopotamia (southern Iraq), roughly 2000-1800 BCE by common estimates — though exact dates are uncertain and not specified in the Qur'an.

**Q: Did Ibrahim really not know who God was at first?**
A: No — the star/moon/sun passage was a *demonstration* for his people. Allah had already guided him (6:75). Prophets are protected from idolatry.

**Q: Why does the Qur'an call his father "Azar" when the Bible says "Terah"?**
A: "Azar" is the Qur'anic name (possibly a second name, title, or epithet). The discrepancy is a historical detail, not a doctrinal problem (§9).

**Q: Is it disrespectful that Ibrahim opposed his own father?**
A: He opposed his father's *idolatry* with the utmost *respect* ("O my father..."). Islam commands kindness to parents — but obedience to them does not extend to disobeying Allah (cf. 29:8; 31:15).
