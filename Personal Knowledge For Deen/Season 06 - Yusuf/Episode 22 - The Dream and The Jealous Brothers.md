# Season 06 · Episode 22
# The Dream & The Jealous Brothers
### Yusuf, the Vision of the Stars, and a Family Torn by Envy

> *"O my father, indeed I have seen [in a dream] eleven stars and the sun and the moon; I saw them prostrating to me."*
> — **Qur'an, Surah Yusuf (12:4)**

---

## How To Use This Chapter

The prophetic line now flows through Ibrahim → Ishaq → Ya'qub (Jacob) to **Yusuf (Joseph), peace be upon him.** His story is unique in the Qur'an: told almost entirely in **one continuous surah (Surah Yusuf, ch. 12)**, which Allah calls *"the best of stories" (ahsan al-qasas).* This first chapter covers the boyhood dream, the burning envy of his brothers, and the plot that ripped him from his father — the opening of a saga about patience, destiny, and the triumph of righteousness over treachery.

> 📖 **Method note:** Because the story is a single, complete, divinely narrated surah, the Qur'an itself is overwhelmingly our source — with far less reliance on hadith or tradition than other seasons. We stay close to the text.

---

## 1. Episode Summary

Yusuf was the beloved younger son of the Prophet **Ya'qub (Jacob)**, himself son of Ishaq, son of Ibrahim — a chain of prophethood. As a boy, Yusuf saw a remarkable dream: **eleven stars, the sun, and the moon prostrating to him.** When he told his father, the wise Ya'qub immediately recognized it as a sign of a great future — and warned Yusuf **not to tell his brothers**, fearing their envy and Shaytan's incitement.

That envy was already simmering. Yusuf and his full brother (Binyamin/Benjamin) were especially beloved to Ya'qub, and the ten older half-brothers resented it bitterly: *"Yusuf and his brother are more beloved to our father than we, while we are a strong clan."* Driven by jealousy, they conspired — first proposing to **kill** Yusuf or cast him away "so that the favour of your father will be exclusively for you," then settling (at one brother's urging) on throwing him into the bottom of a **well**, to be picked up by a passing caravan.

They tricked Ya'qub into letting Yusuf accompany them, then carried out the plot, returning at night **weeping**, with Yusuf's shirt **stained with false blood**, claiming a wolf had devoured him. Ya'qub, a prophet, saw through the lie but responded with **"beautiful patience" (*sabrun jamil*)**. This chapter explores the dream, the anatomy of envy, the plot, and Ya'qub's grief — setting up Yusuf's long journey through slavery, temptation, and prison to ultimate power.

## 3. Background: The Lineage and "The Best of Stories"

> ✅ **Surah Yusuf (12:3):** "We relate to you the best of stories (*ahsan al-qasas*) through what We have revealed to you of this Qur'an, although you were, before it, among the unaware."
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir):** Allah Himself calls Yusuf's story "the best of stories" — for its beauty, completeness, and the depth of its lessons on patience, chastity, forgiveness, family, and destiny. It was revealed to comfort the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ during his own years of persecution and betrayal (by his own people/relatives) — Yusuf's story of being wronged by family yet ultimately triumphant was a direct consolation.

**The lineage:** Yusuf was the son of **Ya'qub** (Jacob, also called *Isra'il* — hence "Children of Israel," his descendants), son of **Ishaq** (Isaac), son of **Ibrahim**. The Prophet ﷺ honoured this chain: *"The noble, son of the noble, son of the noble, son of the noble: Yusuf, son of Ya'qub, son of Ishaq, son of Ibrahim"* (**Sahih al-Bukhari 3390**). Four generations of prophethood in one line.

## 4. The Story

### 4.1 The Dream

> ✅ **Surah Yusuf (12:4-6)**
>
> **Translation:** "When Yusuf said to his father, 'O my father, indeed I have seen [in a dream] eleven stars and the sun and the moon; I saw them prostrating to me.' He said, 'O my son, do not relate your vision to your brothers, lest they contrive against you a plan. Indeed, Shaytan is to man a manifest enemy. And thus will your Lord choose you and teach you the interpretation of narrations/dreams and complete His favour upon you and upon the family of Ya'qub, as He completed it upon your forefathers Ibrahim and Ishaq...'"
>
> **Tafsir:** The dream foretold Yusuf's exalted future — the eleven stars (his eleven brothers), the sun and moon (his parents) all honouring him (fulfilled at the story's end, Episode 25). Ya'qub's response shows **prophetic wisdom**: he recognized the dream's greatness but immediately foresaw the **danger of envy** and counselled secrecy. A timeless lesson: *do not broadcast your blessings and aspirations to those who may envy them* — the Prophet ﷺ taught, "Seek help in fulfilling your needs by concealing them, for everyone who has a blessing is envied" (a reported meaning). Ya'qub also named the real instigator behind envy: **Shaytan, the manifest enemy** (linking back to Season 01).

### 4.2 The Anatomy of Envy

> ✅ **Surah Yusuf (12:8-9)**
>
> **Translation:** "When they said, 'Yusuf and his brother are more beloved to our father than we, while we are a clan (strong group). Indeed, our father is in clear error. Kill Yusuf or cast him out to [another] land; the face (favour) of your father will [then] be only for you, and you will be after that a righteous people.'"
>
> **Tafsir (as-Sa'di):** The brothers' reasoning is a chilling study in how **envy corrupts the mind**:
> - They acknowledged the truth (their father loved Yusuf more) but blamed *him* (the father) and *Yusuf*, not their own hearts.
> - They contemplated **murder** of their own innocent young brother — to *monopolize their father's affection.*
> - Most disturbingly, they planned to **"be righteous afterward"** — imagining they could commit a terrible sin and simply repent later, treating repentance as a loophole. (A warning the Qur'an records: sin planned with "I'll repent after" is itself a deception of Shaytan.)
> Envy (*hasad*) — the sin of Iblis (S1E4) and of Adam's first son (S2E9) — here nearly destroys a prophet's family. It is the recurring poison of the human heart.

### 4.3 The Well

> ✅ **Surah Yusuf (12:10):** "One among them said, 'Do not kill Yusuf but throw him into the bottom of the well; some travelers will pick him up — if you would do [something].'"
>
> **Tafsir:** One brother (the "least bad") proposed sparing his life — abandonment instead of murder. This small mercy within a great cruelty is what kept Yusuf alive for his destiny. They then **manipulated their father** with feigned concern ("why do you not trust us with Yusuf? Let him come play with us; we will protect him" — 12:11-12), and Ya'qub reluctantly agreed, voicing his fear: "I fear a wolf may eat him while you are heedless" (12:13) — ironically handing them their cover story.

### 4.4 The False Blood and "Beautiful Patience"

> ✅ **Surah Yusuf (12:16-18)**
>
> **Translation:** "And they came to their father at night, weeping. They said, 'O our father, we went racing and left Yusuf with our belongings, and a wolf ate him... ' And they brought upon his shirt false blood. [Ya'qub] said, 'Rather, your souls have enticed you to something, so [my course is] beautiful patience (*sabrun jamil*). And Allah is the one sought for help against that which you describe.'"
>
> **Tafsir (Ibn Kathir):** The brothers' theatrical weeping and the **blood-stained shirt** were meant to deceive — but they forgot to tear the shirt, so Ya'qub, with a prophet's insight, knew a wolf could not eat a boy without ripping his clothes. He saw the lie. Yet his response is one of the most beautiful in the Qur'an: ***sabrun jamil*** — "beautiful patience": patience without complaint to creation, only turning to Allah for help. He did not rage, curse, or despair; he grieved with dignity and entrusted his pain to Allah. This phrase became a cornerstone of Islamic teaching on enduring loss.

## 6. Lessons

1. **Guard your blessings and dreams from the envious.** Ya'qub's first instinct was protective secrecy (12:5). Not everyone should hear your aspirations and gifts; envy is real and dangerous.
2. **Envy destroys families and corrupts reason.** The brothers' jealousy led them to contemplate murdering a child. Unchecked *hasad* makes people justify the unthinkable — the same sin that ruined Iblis.
3. **"I'll repent later" is a satanic trap.** The brothers planned to sin and "be righteous after" (12:9). Deliberately sinning on the assumption of future repentance is a deception.
4. **"Beautiful patience" is the believer's response to loss.** Ya'qub grieved deeply yet complained only to Allah (12:18; cf. 12:86). *Sabrun jamil* — dignified patience, trust in Allah — is the model for every heartbreak.
5. **Allah's plans unfold through apparent disasters.** Being thrown into a well looked like the end of Yusuf — it was actually the first step of his rise. What seems like ruin may be the doorway to destiny.

## 7. Misconceptions — Corrected

> ❌ **"Yusuf's brothers were not from a prophet's family / this is just folklore."**
> ✅ They were the sons of the Prophet Ya'qub — and the story is divinely narrated as "the best of stories" (12:3), a real history with the deepest lessons.

> ❌ **"Ya'qub's preference for Yusuf justified the brothers' jealousy."**
> ✅ Parental affection (especially for a young, righteous child showing signs of greatness) did not justify envy or attempted murder. The fault was entirely in the brothers' *hasad*, not in Yusuf or Ya'qub.

> ❌ **"Ya'qub was fooled by the blood-stained shirt."**
> ✅ As a prophet, he saw through it (the unripped shirt betrayed the lie); he chose *sabrun jamil*, not gullibility.

## 8. Weak / Isra'iliyyat Narrations (Labelled)

---
**⚠️ Isra'iliyyat 1 — Names of the brothers, the wolf's "speech," exact ages, the well's location**

- **Narration:** Detailed names of all ten brothers, a story that they brought a captured wolf which "spoke" to deny eating Yusuf, the precise location and depth of the well, Yusuf's exact age.
- **Source:** Isra'iliyyat and tradition; the Qur'an names neither the brothers (except Binyamin by implication) nor these details.
- **Rely on it?** ⚠️ Not established. The Qur'anic narrative is complete; these are embellishments.

## 12. Questions People Usually Ask (FAQ)

**Q: Why is Surah Yusuf called "the best of stories"?**
A: For its beauty, its single complete narrative, and its profound lessons on patience, chastity, forgiveness, family, and trust in Allah's plan (12:3).

**Q: How many brothers did Yusuf have?**
A: Eleven (the "eleven stars" of his dream) — ten older half-brothers and his full younger brother Binyamin. Their names are not given in the Qur'an.

**Q: Did Ya'qub really believe the wolf story?**
A: No — as a prophet, he recognized the lie (the unripped shirt) and responded with "beautiful patience," entrusting the matter to Allah (12:18).

**Q: Why didn't Allah stop the brothers from harming Yusuf?**
A: Because it was the **means** of fulfilling Yusuf's destiny. The Qur'an later shows Yusuf himself recognizing this — what looked like cruelty was the path to his elevation (Episode 25). Allah's wisdom works through trials.
